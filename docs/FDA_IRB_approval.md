IRB (Institutional Review Board) Clinical History
-------------------------------------------------

While it does not have a Compassionate Use footprint, *Curcuma longa* has an extensive **IRB history** spanning the last several decades in American academic and clinical research. [[1](https://journalofethics.ama-assn.org/article/history-and-role-institutional-review-boards-local-and-central-irbs-single-mission/2009-04)]

Any time an American university or hospital wants to study curcumin's effects on humans, they must first obtain approval from an [Institutional Review Board (IRB) to ensure patient safety and ethical oversight](https://www.hpu.edu/oaa/institutional-review-board/history.html). [[1](https://journalofethics.ama-assn.org/article/history-and-role-institutional-review-boards-local-and-central-irbs-single-mission/2009-04)]

Major milestones in U.S. IRB-approved curcumin research include:

Phase I Safety and Dose-Escalation Trials (Late 1990s--2000s)

Early IRB-approved studies focused heavily on safety. Landmark trials at institutions like the [University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center](https://www.mdanderson.org/) established that humans could tolerate massive doses---up to **8,000 to 12,000 mg per day** of oral curcumin---with almost zero toxic side effects. However, these same IRBs noted that almost no curcumin made it into the bloodstream due to poor absorption (low bioavailability).

Oncology and Chemoprevention Studies (2000s--Present)

U.S. medical boards have approved dozens of pilot trials investigating curcumin as an adjunct to cancer therapy.

-   **Colorectal Cancer:** IRBs have overseen trials tracking curcumin's ability to reduce aberrant crypt foci (precancerous lesions) in the colon.
-   **Prostate Cancer:** Active trials (such as [NCT03769766](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT03769766)) look at using curcumin to slow down disease progression in low-risk, localized prostate cancer patients undergoing active surveillance.

Managing Chronic Inflammation (2010s--Present)

Modern IRB oversight often focuses on curcumin formulations engineered with "enhancers" (like black pepper extract piperine or liposomal delivery systems) to bypass the bioavailability barrier. Approved human trials regularly investigate its impact on: [[1](https://journalofethics.ama-assn.org/article/history-and-role-institutional-review-boards-local-and-central-irbs-single-mission/2009-04)]

-   **Neurology:** Modulating cognitive decline and amyloid plaque buildup in [Alzheimer's disease trials](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3580400/).
-   **Autoimmune & Inflammation:** Managing joint inflammation in osteoarthritis and bowel inflammation in ulcerative colitis.
-   **Hematology:** Evaluating symptoms and inflammatory markers in pre-malignant bone marrow disorders (such as [NCT06063486](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06063486)).
